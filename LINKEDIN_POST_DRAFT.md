# LinkedIn launch post — draft

Three versions below at decreasing lengths. LinkedIn shows the first ~210 characters before "...see more" — make sure the hook lands in that window.

---

## Version A — Long (~1700 chars, ~280 words)

I spent the last few months building a free dashboard that tracks how exposed every country in the world is to food shocks. It's live now and I'd love to know what you think.

I'm a student at Erasmus University in Rotterdam, not a food-security expert. I built it because I got curious: if something goes wrong in one country tomorrow — a war, a drought, a port closure — who actually gets hurt, and how badly?

The data exists. FAO has prices. WFP has hunger. World Bank has trade. NASA has satellite weather. ACLED has conflict events. Each of them is excellent. None of them, by themselves, answers that question.

So I started wiring them together. One pipeline became three. Three became ten. It ended up at 33 public data feeds, refreshing every six hours, covering 193 countries and all 50 US states, distilled into a single 0-100 score with every input traceable to its source.

Three things surprised me while building it:

→ The world's food trade is shockingly concentrated. A handful of suppliers feed most of the countries currently in humanitarian crisis.

→ The "structurally stable" countries — Egypt, Lebanon, Tunisia — sit one shock away from a serious problem.

→ Most of the useful data is technically free, but practically locked behind APIs, PDFs, and spreadsheets nobody reads. The synthesis is the product.

It's called FoodShield AI. It's free, open, and honest about what it knows and doesn't know. Every number is labeled live or modelled.

→ https://foodshield-ai-fv.vercel.app/

The question I'd most love an answer to: could companies actually use this? If you're in procurement, risk, supply chain, ESG, food, or agri — I'd genuinely love to hear how you'd put a tool like this to work. Or what's missing for it to be useful.

## Notes on which one to use

- **Version A** is the most "founder note" — best if you want the post to be a substantive read, not a sound bite. Reads like a story.
- **Version B** is the safest LinkedIn middle. Hooks in the first sentence, lists the surprise points, ends with the ask.
- **Version C** is the "fast scroll" version. Good if your network is mostly going to be exposed via mobile feed and you're optimizing for clicks-through.

My recommendation: **Version B**. Long enough to feel real, short enough to actually be read.

## Posting checklist (before you hit publish)

1. **Image**: attach the og-image.png as a custom image — LinkedIn renders custom uploads bigger than auto-fetched OG previews. Or post the link first, let the og:image auto-render, then *also* attach a separate screenshot of a Yemen or Egypt country panel for a second visual.
2. **First-comment trick**: don't put the URL in the body if you want maximum reach — LinkedIn deprioritizes posts with external links. Put it in the first comment instead, and write "→ Link in first comment" in the body.
3. **Tag thoughtfully**: 3–5 specific people whose work you reference (e.g. someone you follow at WFP, FAO, or a food-security researcher you respect). Avoid spraying @WFP / @FAO accounts — those rarely engage, and the algorithm penalises spray-tagging.
4. **Post timing**: weekday morning Europe time (8:30–10:30 CET) catches both Europe and East-Coast US.
5. **First-hour engagement**: ask 2–3 friends you trust to comment substantively (not just "great post!") within the first hour. LinkedIn weighs early-comment quality heavily.
6. **Reply to every comment in the first 24h**. The algorithm rewards comment-thread depth more than likes.

## Things to be ready to answer in comments

- "How is this different from FAO GIEWS / WFP HungerMap / FEWS NET?" — Be ready: those are domain-expert tools with rich detail per country; FoodShield synthesises across all of them into one comparable score.
- "Where does the data come from?" — Methodology page; every metric is sourced.
- "What's the business model?" — Don't have one, built it as a learning project. Honest is better than vague.
- "Why a student? Are you qualified?" — Lean in. "I'm not. I built it because no one else had, and curiosity is a reasonable starting point. The data and methodology are all public — judge the work, not the credentials."
- "Did you use AI?" — Yes, as a tool. The data is all real public-source data. AI helped with code and synthesis, not the underlying facts.
